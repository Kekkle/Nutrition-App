# Writing Style Guide

Derived from the tone, voice, and phrasing used in the Canva presentation (100 slides). Use this guide when writing any in-app lesson text, blurbs, game prompts, or educational copy.

---

## Voice and persona

**Who is speaking?** A friendly, curious guide who talks *with* kids, not *at* them. Think of a cool older sibling or camp counselor who genuinely thinks science is awesome and assumes you do too.

**Point of view:** Second person ("your body," "you might be wondering") mixed with first-person-plural ("our phones," "our bodies," "let's explore"). This makes the reader feel included rather than lectured.

---

## Tone qualities

| Quality | How it shows up |
|---------|-----------------|
| **Conversational** | Short sentences, contractions ("let's," "they're," "we're"), sentence fragments used for dramatic pacing ("Cells!") |
| **Curious and wondering** | Questions appear frequently to model curiosity: "But what decides whether a food is a 'protein' or a 'carb' or a 'fat'?" and "So how does glucose get into cells?" |
| **Playful** | Anthropomorphised characters (insulin says "Sup?", cells introduce themselves: "I'm a neuron!", "We're epithelial cells!"), humorous phrasing ("needy cells") |
| **Encouraging** | Celebratory reveals ("That's right, carbohydrates!"), positive reinforcement, never shaming |
| **Building suspense** | Deliberate use of ellipses and split-across-slides reveals: "...an energy source is needed," "to release a hormone called... ...insulin." |

---

## Sentence structure

- **Keep sentences short.** Aim for 5--15 words. Max of two clauses.
- **One idea per sentence.** Don't cram digestion steps into a single long sentence.
- **Use fragments intentionally.** "Cells!" / "Energy!" / "Much like a phone." These create punchy moments of emphasis.
- **Split long ideas across beats.** Rather than: "Insulin opens a portal on the cell to allow glucose to enter," break it into: "Insulin is like a key." / "It opens a portal on the cell..." / "...to allow glucose to enter."

---

## Vocabulary rules

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Use simple, everyday words ("tiny," "break down," "travels") | Avoid jargon-heavy phrasing without context |
| Introduce science terms naturally alongside a plain-language definition ("a hormone called insulin," "tiny fat droplets called lipids") | Drop a term without explaining what it means |
| Use relatable analogies ("insulin is like a key," "your body is like a phone," "body systems = apps") | Use abstract explanations with no anchor to something kids know |
| Say "energy" or "power" | Say "calories" |
| Say "nourishment" or "what your body needs" | Say "diet" or "weight" |
| Say "more supportive" / "less supportive" when comparing foods | Say "good food" / "bad food" |

---

## Analogy framework

The presentation leans heavily on **sustained analogies** -- a single metaphor that carries across many slides. Keep this approach:

1. **Phone analogy** -- body systems are apps, food is the charger, electricity = energy from food.
2. **Key-and-lock analogy** -- insulin is a key that opens a portal on the cell so glucose can enter.
3. **Magnifying-glass motif** -- "let's take a closer look" used to zoom from macro (food, body) to micro (molecules, cells).

When introducing a new concept, anchor it to something the audience already knows before naming the scientific term.

---

## Question-driven flow

The presentation models a "wondering out loud" pattern. Replicate this:

1. **Pose the question** the learner is probably thinking: "You might be wondering: if we get our energy from carbs, why do we even need fats and protein?"
2. **Pause for curiosity** ("Hmm... Wait a second...")
3. **Build toward the answer** step by step.
4. **Confirm and celebrate** ("That's right, carbohydrates!")

This pattern turns passive reading into active thinking.

---

## Interactive prompts

The presentation frequently asks the reader to participate before revealing the answer:

- "Can you come up with some examples?"
- "Ideas of protein foods?"
- "What foods might those be?"
- "Can you tell what it is mostly made up of?"

In the app, these should map to tap/drag game moments or quiz prompts that let the learner guess before the reveal.

---

## Pacing and rhythm

- **Use section dividers** (the presentation uses numbered title cards: "1.", "2.", "3.", etc.) to signal a new chapter.
- **Build from familiar to new.** Every section starts with something the learner already knows (phones, food they eat) and zooms into something new (molecules, cells, insulin).
- **End sections with a bridge.** Before moving on, explicitly connect back: "Now we're ready to explore how food turns into energy!" This prevents the feeling of random topic jumps.

---

## Character and personality notes

- **Insulin** is a character with sunglasses and a laid-back attitude ("Sup?"). Keep insulin cool and helpful.
- **Glucose molecules** are small, cute, pentagon-shaped characters with faces.
- **Cells** introduce themselves by name and job: "I'm a neuron!", "I'm an osteocyte!", "We're epithelial cells!"
- **The phone** is a kawaii-style character with blushing cheeks and little arms.
- **The thinking figure** appears when a question is posed -- stick figure with a green question mark and thought bubble.
- **A purple fuzzy blob creature** appears periodically as a mascot-like companion.

---

## Formatting conventions

- **Headings** use bold, expressive, uppercase display font (maps to Lilita One in the app). Only the app title is fully uppercase; section headings use sentence case.
- **Body/blurb text** appears on purple/magenta highlight banners in the presentation. In the app, use the Quicksand font for this text.
- **Key terms** are introduced in a highlighted or contrasting color (e.g., "MACRO NUTRIENTS" in purple against white heading text).
- **Equations and equivalences** are shown visually: "FOOD = ENERGY," "Each system = an app." Use this visual shorthand in the app too.

---

## Content guardrails

These rules are non-negotiable across all copy:

- **No "good food" / "bad food" language.** All foods provide something. Use "more supportive" vs "less supportive" if comparing.
- **No calorie talk.** Always say "energy" or "nourishment."
- **No weight or body-size commentary.** Never frame nutrition as a path to changing body shape.
- **No shame for wrong answers.** Game feedback should encourage: "Not quite -- try again!" or "Almost! Here's a hint."
- **Food neutrality.** Potato chips, cookies, croissants all appear alongside fruits and vegetables. No food is villainized.
